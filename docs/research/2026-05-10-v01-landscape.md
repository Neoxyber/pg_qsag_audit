## Orientation

This document is a research synthesis prepared to inform the v0.1 implementation of `pg_qsag_audit`, the third public artefact in the Q-SAG open-source substrate programme. It surveys the cryptographic, regulatory, and PostgreSQL-extension landscape across the 2027–2030 horizon and identifies three concrete Stage 0 hardenings that should land in ADR-0001 before any v0.1 implementation code is written.

This document is **not** a marketing piece, **not** a position paper, **not** legal advice, and **not** a substitute for ADR-0001 itself. It is a sourced reference compiled from primary documents (NIST publications, IETF drafts, EU Regulation texts in the Official Journal, academic papers, peer-reviewed conference proceedings, and GitHub repository state) intended to make the v0.1 design defensible to a technical reviewer who asks "what is your basis for this choice?".

The synthesis was prepared under instruction to be exhaustive. Length is intentional. Section structure is loosely modelled on a NIST Special Publication: TL;DR, Key Findings (twelve numbered sections), Per-Topic Implementation Implications, Staged Recommendations, Caveats. Each section carries its own footnotes; the convention is `[^N]` markers in prose, with `[^N]: Author/Source. "Title". Date. URL.` definitions collected at the end of each section.

## TL;DR

- **The ADR-0001 design is fundamentally sound and defensible through 2027.** SHA3-384 as the chain-hash is the right call for a 2027–2030-horizon AI-agent audit substrate: it is the only NIST-standard hash that retains a full 128-bit collision floor against the Brassard–Høyer–Tapp quantum bound, it is in CNSA 2.0's "limited internal use" allow-list for hardware components, and it is required for SCITT/COSE Receipts interoperability paths via SHAKE/KMAC siblings. Do **not** swap to SHA-384 (classical SHA-2) at v0.1 even though CNSA 2.0 prefers it for external NSS interfaces — the substrate's threat model is post-quantum durability, and CNSA 2.0 explicitly permits SHA3-384 internally.

- **The single largest 2027 risk to the substrate is *not* cryptographic; it is regulatory churn.** The EU Digital Omnibus on AI (proposed 19 November 2025) is in active trilogue and would defer EU AI Act Article 12 high-risk obligations from 2 August 2026 to *up to* 2 December 2027 (stand-alone systems) or 2 August 2028 (embedded systems). As of the trilogue impasse on 28 April 2026 the original 2 August 2026 deadline still stands. Architect ADR-0001 §2.6 reservations to absorb a 2027 deferral *without* re-architecture, but ship v0.1 against the *current* statutory text.

- **The dispatcher (CSWP-39 §3.2.3) and the chain-linkage formula (`H_n = SHA3-384(H_{n-1} || H_event_n)`) need three concrete v0.1 hardenings:**
 1. The dispatcher must log the *negotiated* algorithm identifier (`alg_id`) into every audit row (CSWP-39 §3.2.3 negotiation-integrity binding), not only at session start.
 2. The chain-link must include an explicit algorithm-tag domain separator inside `dsep_hash` so a future SHA3-512 / SLH-DSA / KMAC migration cannot collide pre-migration and post-migration roots — specifically `dsep_hash = SHA3-384(b"q-sag-audit:v1:" || alg_id || canonical_payload)`.
 3. The pure-plpgsql fallback must be KAT-pinned against the FIPS 202 example file *and* the Keccak team's long-message vector (~1 GB, or sampled at 10 MB minimum) at CI time, *and* a rate-boundary vector at exactly 832 bits (104 bytes) for SHA3-384, because that is the vector class that has historically caught lane-indexing and pad10*1 transcription errors in interpreted-language ports.

These three hardenings are *tightenings* of the existing ADR-0001 design, not reversals. The substrate design holds.

## Key Findings

### 1. Cryptographic baseline is stable enough to build on

- **FIPS 202 (August 2015)** has one acknowledged typographical erratum in Appendix B Algorithm 10 step 1 (an off-by-one indexing slip; non-normative).[^1-1] NIST's Crypto Publication Review Board re-opened FIPS 202 in July 2023 and announced in March 2025 a decision to (a) update FIPS 202 for editorial quality only — withdrawing SHA-1 / 3DES references, no algorithmic changes — and (b) revise SP 800-185 to add *streaming* SHAKE128 / SHAKE256 specifications.[^1-2] **No SHA-3 algorithmic change is on the table.** The Keccak team (Bertoni, Daemen, Peeters, Van Assche, Van Keer) have publicly confirmed that Keccak-f[1600] has not changed since round 2 of the 2008–2012 SHA-3 competition.[^1-3] Implementations matching the 2015 KATs remain bit-exact correct.

- **NIST CAVP / KAT corpus.** The authoritative example/KAT files live under `csrc.nist.gov/projects/cryptographic-algorithm-validation-program/secure-hashing` and the historical Keccak Known Answer Tests (KAT/MCT/Long files). They are U.S. Government works, public domain. The vectors that have *empirically* caught bugs in interpreted-language ports are:
 - empty string;
 - the canonical "abc" vector;
 - the 448-bit and 896-bit boundary vectors (one-block / two-block padding behaviour);
 - the 1,000,000 × 'a' Monte-Carlo vector (catches MCT seed bugs);
 - the long ~1 GB extreme-message vector that exercises the rate boundary at 1088 bits (SHA3-256), 832 bits (SHA3-384) and 576 bits (SHA3-512).

 The classic interpreted-port bug classes are:
 - **bit-rotation offset matrix transcription** — the 5×5 ρ offsets — most pure-language ports get one cell wrong;
 - **round-constant typo** in 1–2 of the 24 ι constants;
 - **lane-indexing convention** — Keccak uses `A[x,y]` with little-endian byte order; column-major vs row-major mistakes are common in plpgsql/SQL;
 - **padding rule** — `pad10*1` with the FIPS 202 domain-separation byte **0x06** for SHA-3, vs **0x1F** for SHAKE, vs **0x01** for raw Keccak (Ethereum) — a port that uses 0x01 will silently produce *Ethereum-style* hashes that pass internal self-consistency tests but fail FIPS 202 KATs;
 - **rate boundary off-by-one** when message length is exactly equal to rate.

- **SP 800-185** (KMAC, TupleHash, ParallelHash, cSHAKE) is being revised (decision March 2025, draft expected late 2025 / 2026) to add streaming variants.[^1-2] KMAC is the relevant primitive for v0.5 if a keyed-MAC chain mode is added; the streaming addition is forward-compatible and does not invalidate v0.1 KMAC vectors.

- **NIST SP 800-232 Ascon-based Lightweight Cryptography** (Ascon-AEAD128, Ascon-Hash256, Ascon-XOF128, Ascon-CXOF128) was finalised on 13 August 2025.[^1-4] Ascon is targeted at IoT / constrained devices (RFID, implants, sensors).[^1-5] **For pg_qsag_audit, Ascon is not a v0.1 candidate** — the substrate runs on managed Postgres, where AES/SHA-3 hardware paths dominate. Ascon-Hash256 has only 128-bit collision security (vs SHA3-384's 192-bit classical / 128-bit BHT-quantum floor). Add Ascon to v0.5 *only* if the substrate ever extends to TPM/HSM-side anchoring on constrained hardware; otherwise out-of-scope.

- **NIST CSWP 39 "Considerations for Achieving Crypto Agility" (final 19 December 2025).** Section 3.2.3 ("Integrity for Algorithm Negotiation") is the load-bearing reference for the ADR-0001 dispatcher.[^1-6] Its substantive mandate: cryptographic algorithm selection or negotiation **must be integrity-protected**; absent that, protocols suffer downgrade attacks. CSWP 39 explicitly warns that when a single mandatory-to-implement algorithm protects negotiation, "eventually that single algorithm will be found to be weak", and the integrity-protection algorithm must be **at least as strong as the next set of algorithms it negotiates**.

 **Implication for ADR-0001:** the dispatcher's pgcrypto-vs-fallback routing decision is itself a negotiation event — it must be (a) logged with an algorithm identifier into the audit chain, (b) authenticated by the chain hash so a downgrade from SHA3-384 (pgcrypto) to a fallback path cannot be silently rewritten, and (c) accompanied by policy/mechanism separation (CSWP 39's recommended pattern: cryptographic policy in config, not code).

- **CNSA 2.0** (NSA Commercial National Security Algorithm Suite 2.0). Procurement gate: 1 January 2027 — all new National Security System acquisitions must be CNSA 2.0 compliant.[^1-7] Algorithms: ML-KEM-1024 (FIPS 203) for KEM, ML-DSA-87 (FIPS 204) for signatures, AES-256, SHA-384/SHA-512 for hashing, LMS/XMSS (SP 800-208) for software/firmware signing.[^1-8] Critical nuance for pg_qsag_audit: **SLH-DSA (FIPS 205) is NOT approved for CNSA 2.0**, and **SHA3-384 / SHA3-512 are permitted only for "internal hardware components that do not interface with external systems"** (e.g., secure boot, integrity checks within a vendor-controlled boundary).[^1-9] CNSA 2.0 prefers classical SHA-384 for external NSS interfaces.

 **Implication for ADR-0001:** SHA3-384 remains correct for the *internal* audit-chain primitive (which is exactly the CNSA 2.0 internal-component carve-out); but if Q-SAG ever pursues NSS-adjacent procurement, an externally-facing manifest signature must be SHA-384 + ML-DSA-87, not SHA3-384. This is a v0.5 SCITT-emitter design constraint, not a v0.1 problem.

- **Quantum cryptanalysis state of the art.** Brassard–Høyer–Tapp (BHT, 1997) gives O(N^(1/3)) quantum collision-finding queries.[^1-10] Concretely: SHA-256 / SHA3-256 with 128-bit classical collision security drops to ~85-bit BHT-quantum security; SHA-384 / SHA3-384 with 192-bit classical drops to ~128-bit BHT-quantum.[^1-11] In other words, SHA3-384 is the *minimum* hash that preserves the 128-bit post-quantum collision floor that CNSA 2.0 / NIST PQC Cat-3 demands. The 2025 academic literature[^1-12] [^1-13] advances classical/quantum reduced-round attacks on Keccak (now 6-round collisions on SHA3-256 / SHAKE128) but **full 24-round Keccak-f[1600] remains unbroken**; expert consensus places a cryptographically-relevant quantum computer attacking SHA3-384 well outside the 2030 horizon. NSA's "by 2030" guidance is about RSA / ECC, not symmetric / hash. The IETF PQUIP working group's harvest-now-decrypt-later concern *does not apply to hashes* — it applies to public-key encryption.

- **PQC implementation maturity (May 2026).** OpenSSL 3.5 LTS (released April 2025, supported through 2030) ships ML-KEM, ML-DSA, SLH-DSA natively. OpenSSL 4.0 (released April 2026) extends this.[^1-14] RustCrypto provides pure-Rust `ml-dsa`, `ml-kem`, `slh-dsa` crates (none independently audited as of May 2026; all KAT-tested against NIST vectors).[^1-15] liboqs supports ML-KEM and ML-DSA as final standards (its README still warns "DO NOT RELY ON THIS LIBRARY IN A PRODUCTION ENVIRONMENT" as of May 2026); Falcon / FN-DSA tracks Round 3, not finalised.[^1-16] PyCA `cryptography` adds PQC bindings via OpenSSL 3.5+ provider model. Hash-based signatures (XMSS / LMS per SP 800-208, SPHINCS+ / SLH-DSA per FIPS 205) are production-ready in liboqs and OpenSSL 3.5+.

- **Constant-time SHA-3 in interpreted languages.** SHA-3 / Keccak is *naturally* constant-time at the algorithmic level — the round function uses only XOR, AND, NOT, and rotations, with no data-dependent branches or memory accesses. The risk in plpgsql is *not* SHA-3 itself but the surrounding glue (input length-handling, padding application, hex/bytea conversion). pgrx-native Rust can call the `sha3` crate's `Sha3_384` which is constant-time by construction. The pure-plpgsql fallback should treat SHA-3 as constant-time and *avoid* introducing data-dependent branches in pad10*1 application; this is a code-review checklist item, not a fundamental obstacle.

### 2. PostgreSQL extension ecosystem

- **PostgreSQL 18** (released 25 September 2025; current minor 18.3 as of 26 February 2026).[^2-1] Async I/O subsystem (`io_method`), `uuidv7()` (RFC 9562), virtual generated columns by default, OAuth 2.0 authentication, OLD/NEW in RETURNING, statistics-preserving `pg_upgrade` with `--swap`, MD5 password deprecation, `extension_control_path` GUC, dynamic extension loading, access methods for new index types.[^2-2] [^2-3]

 **Material to pg_qsag_audit:** the `extension_control_path` GUC dramatically simplifies pg_tle and pgrx flavour coexistence; `uuidv7()` is timestamp-orderable and fits well as an event-id field; OLD/NEW RETURNING enables single-statement before/after audit capture. PG18 deprecates MD5 — relevant only to legacy fallback paths.

- **PostgreSQL 19** is in development; feature freeze 8 April 2026; release planned September 2026.[^2-4] [^2-5] C requirement raised to C11. Parallel autovacuum, parallel TID range scans, parallel GIN builds, async I/O worker auto-scaling. No known extension-author-breaking ABI change identified to date for v19.

- **PostgreSQL 20** (2027 cycle) trajectory is speculative; expect continued ABI stability based on the 2025 ABI-policy patch by Peter Eisentraut.

- **pg_tle 1.5.0** (released 19 March 2026) drops PG11 support; combined v1.4.1 + v1.5.0; adds `application_name` parameter to `clientauth_port_subset`; updates `CreateQueryDesc` for PG18; adds schema parameter to `install_extension`.[^2-6] AWS RDS for PostgreSQL ships pg_tle 1.5.2 (latest catalogued).[^2-7]

 **Implication:** v0.1's pg_tle flavour should target pg_tle ≥1.5.0 baseline (PG13–PG18) and explicitly mark PG11 unsupported (which it already is via PG18-only feature set).

- **pgrx 0.17.0** (released 9 February 2026, current as of 1 April 2026 with 0.16.1 still active branch) supports PG13–PG18, requires Rust 1.88.0+, migrated to Rust edition 2024.[^2-8] [^2-9] pgrx remains pre-1.0 by deliberate choice — there is no MSRV policy and breaking changes between minor versions remain possible.

 **Implication:** the pgrx-native flavour should pin to a single major (recommended: 0.17.x for PG13–PG18 coverage), and v0.1 should plan a pgrx-version bump as a routine maintenance event, not a stability promise.

- **PGXN v2.** RFC-2 (Trunk binary distribution format) and RFC-3 (Meta Spec v2 with contents taxonomy) are open pull requests against `pgxn/rfcs`.[^2-10] [^2-11] RFC-5 (release certification with JWS-signed META.json) has not been formally filed as of May 2026. The Rust SDK `pgxn_meta` v0.1.0 validates v1 and v2 META.json files. **Trunk binary distribution by Tembo** (`pgt.dev`) currently hosts ~237 extensions and predates the RFC-2 spec by ~3 years; the RFC-2 format is consciously aligned with Tembo Trunk's directory layout.[^2-12]

 **Implication for pg_qsag_audit v0.1:** publish a v1 META.json today and shadow with a v2 META.json + Trunk-format `.trunk` bundle once RFC-2 is accepted (expected H2 2026 based on current cadence).

- **Supabase dbdev** continues to support TLE-style publishing via SQL bundles; dual-flavour artefacts (pg_tle + pgrx-native from the same repo) are supported in the sense that dbdev does not reject sibling pgrx packaging, but dbdev's discovery surface is TLE-only.

- **pg_credereum** (PostgresPro) remains a research prototype (last substantive commit cluster 2018–2019, ~55 GitHub stars).[^2-13] Builds a Merkle prefix tree (merklix) over JSON-encoded rows, hashes with **SHA-256**, anchors root hash to **Ethereum smart contract**. *No updates 2024–2026.* Useful as prior-art reference and as a contrast point — pg_qsag_audit's design (per-event chain over canonical payload + dsep_hash, SHA3-384) is materially different and substantively stronger.

- **pgaudit** continues mainline, current with PG18; integration pattern with hash-chain primitives is "pgaudit emits structured event log → pg_qsag_audit canonicalises and chains". This is an obvious v0.5 integration adapter.

- **immudb + Postgres** remain separate projects; FDW-based integration alternatives are not actively standardised.

- **Madhwal et al. (BIOTC 2021, "Blockchain Extension for PostgreSQL Data Storage")** has had no substantive academic follow-up by the same authors; subsequent academic work on PostgreSQL-resident audit chains has migrated to broader "tamper-evident logging" / SCITT / Forestrie literature.

- **Crosby–Wallach 2009 history-tree** (USENIX Security'09, Rice University; Java reference impl at `tamperevident.cs.rice.edu/git/fastsig.git`, GitHub `scrosby/fastsig`) remains the foundational reference.[^2-14] [^2-15] Subsequent refinements: Dowling et al. ePrint 2016/452 ("Secure Logging Schemes and Certificate Transparency") which formally generalised the construction[^2-16], and the resulting RFC 9162 (Certificate Transparency v2.0). For pg_qsag_audit v0.5, **the right Merkle-accumulator design is RFC 9162-style (which generalises Crosby-Wallach), not literal Crosby-Wallach** — RFC 9162 is the verifiable-data-structure used by both Sigstore Rekor and SCITT/COSE Receipts (`draft-ietf-cose-merkle-tree-proofs-18`). Going Crosby-Wallach-exact would isolate the substrate from the SCITT receipt ecosystem.

### 3. OpenSSL and crypto-library landscape

- **OpenSSL 3.0 LTS** EOL 7 September 2026; **OpenSSL 3.2** EOL April 2026 (already past); **OpenSSL 3.5 LTS** released February/April 2025, supported 5 years (regular fixes through 2029, security-only through 2030).[^3-1] **OpenSSL 4.0** GA April 2026 (non-LTS, supported May 2027). **OpenSSL 5.0** planned October 2027 (next LTS). Cadence: major LTS every 2 years from 2025 (3.5 → 5.0 → 7.0).[^3-2]

- **OpenSSL EVP / SHA-3 availability across managed-cloud Postgres tiers (May 2026 snapshot):**
 - **AWS RDS / Aurora PostgreSQL**: pgcrypto links the system OpenSSL of the RDS image; PG14+ on RDS uses OpenSSL 3.x where SHA-3 family digests (SHA3-224/256/384/512) are auto-picked-up via `EVP_get_digestbyname()`. *pgcrypto's `digest()` accepts `'sha3-384'` because OpenSSL 3.x supports it, even though pgcrypto's documented "standard algorithms" list shows only SHA-2.*
 - **Azure Database for PostgreSQL** (Flexible Server): same pattern, OpenSSL 3.x, SHA-3 available.
 - **Google Cloud SQL for PostgreSQL** / **AlloyDB**: OpenSSL 3.x, SHA-3 available.
 - **Supabase**: vanilla OpenSSL 3.x in container, SHA-3 available.
 - **Neon**, **Crunchy Bridge**: OpenSSL 3.x, SHA-3 available.

 **Where the fallback path will fire:** legacy on-prem PG13/14 builds linked against OpenSSL 1.1.1 (which *did not* expose SHA-3 by default), FIPS-stripped builds (rare but extant in some federal/government tiers), and any build where pgcrypto's configure-time `--with-openssl` was off.

- **BoringSSL / LibreSSL / AWS-LC.** AWS-LC is now AWS RDS's default crypto provider for some newer Aurora releases; AWS-LC supports SHA-3. BoringSSL and LibreSSL are not used by mainstream managed-Postgres tiers.

 *Implication:* the dispatcher's crypto-source detection (CSWP-39 §3.2.3 binding) should *not* hardcode "OpenSSL ≥ 3.0" as the predicate; it should probe `digest('test', 'sha3-384')` at session start and route on the result. This is what the ADR-0001 dispatcher already does, validating the design.

- **liboqs** (Open Quantum Safe) v0.13.x as of May 2026; supports ML-KEM-512/768/1024, ML-DSA-44/65/87, SLH-DSA all parameter sets; Falcon tracks Round 3.[^3-3] **Production-readiness disclaimer remains in the README.** Integration into OpenSSL is via the `oqs-provider` provider plug-in (not in-tree). For pg_qsag_audit v0.5+ SCITT signing, prefer OpenSSL 3.5+ native ML-DSA over liboqs+oqs-provider.

### 4. SCITT, COSE Receipts, and transparency-service standards

- **`draft-ietf-scitt-architecture-22`** (17 October 2025; expires 13/20 April 2026; latest of -22 expires 20 April 2026). Authors: Birkholz (Fraunhofer SIT), Delignat-Lavaud, Fournet (Microsoft Research), Deshpande (ARM), Lasker.[^4-1] [^4-2] Defines Issuer / Transparency Service / Auditor / Relying Party roles, Signed Statements (COSE Sign1 envelopes), Receipts (proofs from a Verifiable Data Structure), Transparent Statements, and Registration Policies. **Expected RFC publication: late 2026 / H1 2027** based on draft cadence (-22 in 17 months). Breaking changes between -16 (August 2025) and -22 (October 2025) were minor — refinement of x5chain handling, certification-path validation language, and registration-policy text.

- **`draft-ietf-cose-merkle-tree-proofs-18`** (2 December 2025; expires 5 June 2026). Defines COSE Receipts as CBOR-encoded inclusion / consistency proofs over a verifiable data structure (RFC 9162-style binary Merkle tree by default). Stable since -17.[^4-3] **Expected RFC publication: H2 2026.**

- **`draft-ietf-scitt-scrapi-06`** (29 December 2025; expires 2 July 2026). Defines REST API for SCITT (issue / register / verify Signed Statements; issue / verify Receipts; produce Transparent Statements). Authors: Birkholz, Geater (DataTrails). Stable trajectory.[^4-4]

- **`draft-ietf-scitt-receipts-ccf-profile-01`** (April 2026, expires 4 October 2026) defines the CCF (Confidential Consortium Framework) profile — Microsoft's TEE-backed transparency service uses this.[^4-5]

- **C2SP tlog-cosignature / tlog-witness** specifications (Cosignature Specification Parts) — the spec basis for Sigstore's witness model. The Rekor v2 GA (referenced in Cosign 3.0.1+, Sigstore signing config now mandatory for v2 instances) integrates witnessing directly into the log.

- **Sigstore Rekor v2** is now GA (2025/2026 phased rollout) on a tile-based log (Trillian-Tessera) replacing Trillian.[^4-6] [^4-7] Single endpoint `/api/v2/log/entries`. Only `hashedrekord` and `dsse` entry types. Timestamps now from a dedicated TSA service (not Rekor).[^4-8] Witnessing is integrated.[^4-9] **Roadmap-listed but not yet shipped:** post-quantum signature support (Sigstore is still ECDSA-P256 + RSA in production; ML-DSA discussed in roadmap but not GA).

- **Microsoft CCF SCITT reference** (`microsoft/scitt-ccf-ledger`) is current with architecture draft -22 (Section 4.4 Transparent Statement outputs), COSE Receipt draft -18 (Section 4), and CCF Tree algorithm profile draft -3.[^4-10] It's a research prototype but actively maintained (March 2026 commit activity confirmed). Azure ships a managed offering: `Azure.Security.CodeTransparency` SDK (.NET preview) backed by Azure Confidential Ledger.[^4-11]

- **DataTrails Forestrie** SCITT implementation: continues active development; integrates with the C2SP witness model; production-grade. Geater (DataTrails) is co-author of SCRAPI-06.

- **Transmute Industries** — Orie Steele is co-author of `draft-ietf-cose-merkle-tree-proofs-18` (now affiliated "Tradeverifyd").[^4-12] SCITT work continues.

- **OpenTimestamps** Bitcoin calendar trustworthiness: stable but operationally fragile (small operator pool, calendar server availability concerns). Alternatives for Bitcoin-anchored timestamping: Eternity Wall, Lightning-Network-Time, BTC OP_RETURN direct anchoring. For substrate-grade durability, *prefer RFC 3161 TSA + SCITT receipt* over OpenTimestamps; reserve Bitcoin anchoring for extremely-long-horizon (decadal) integrity.

### 5. EU regulatory landscape — the dominant 2026–2027 risk surface

- **EU AI Act (Regulation 2024/1689)**, full text in OJ L of 12 July 2024.
 - **Article 12 (Record-keeping)** for high-risk AI systems: full force **2 August 2026**.[^5-1] Requires technically automatic event logging "over the lifetime of the system", with three statutory purposes: identifying risk situations under Article 79(1), supporting post-market monitoring (Article 72), and tracking operational performance (Article 26(5)). Article 26(6) imposes a **minimum 6-month retention** on deployers. Article 12 does **not** literally use the word "tamper-proof", but enforcement practice and academic / vendor commentary[^5-2] [^5-3] converge on tamper-evident hash-chained logs as the only compliance-credible architecture — which is exactly what pg_qsag_audit provides.
 - **Article 26 (deployer obligations)** including Article 26(5) (operational monitoring) and Article 26(6) (six-month log retention) bind deployers regardless of whether they built the system.
 - **Annex IV (technical documentation)** export-format requirements remain at the Member-State / harmonised-standard level (CEN-CENELEC JTC 21 work-in-progress). prEN 18229-1 (AI logging and human oversight) and ISO/IEC DIS 24970 (AI system logging) are the two relevant standards drafts; **neither is finalised** as of May 2026.
 - **Penalties:** up to €15 million or 3 % of global turnover for Article 12 / 26 breaches.

- **EU Digital Omnibus on AI** (proposal COM(2025) 836, 19 November 2025).[^5-4] [^5-5] Proposes to link Article 12 / high-risk applicability to standards-availability with a long-stop date of **2 December 2027 for stand-alone high-risk AI systems** and **2 August 2028 for high-risk AI systems embedded in regulated products**.[^5-6] [^5-7] Trilogue: 2nd political trilogue 28 April 2026 ended *without agreement*; 3rd scheduled 13 May 2026; provisional agreement 7 May 2026 reached at Council level (Consilium press release). **Status as of cut-off:** original 2 August 2026 deadline still legally binding; deferral *probable but not certain*. Architect to the original date.

- **EU CRA (Regulation 2024/2847).** Entered into force 10 December 2024.[^5-8] Phased applicability: Chapter IV (notified bodies) **11 June 2026**; **Article 14 reporting cascade enforceable 11 September 2026**;[^5-9] [^5-10] full application 11 December 2027. Article 14 cadence: 24-hour early warning, 72-hour vulnerability notification, 14-day final report (vulnerabilities) / 30-day final report (severe incidents), all via the **ENISA Single Reporting Platform (SRP)**.[^5-11] [^5-12] SRP operational by 11 September 2026; ENISA contracted SRP build to a private vendor; FAQ live at the ENISA SRP topic page. CRA Article 14 applies to *all products on the market*, not only post-December-2027 products (Article 69(3)). Annex II Part II essential cybersecurity requirements include vulnerability handling and SBOM (a "commonly used and machine-readable format covering at least top-level dependencies"). Penalties: up to €15 million / 2.5% of worldwide turnover (Article 64).

- **DORA (Regulation 2022/2554):** full applicability **17 January 2025** — already in force. ICT third-party risk requirements (Articles 28–30) bind financial entities and cascade to ICT providers (including database-tier audit substrates). pg_qsag_audit's hash-chained audit fits the "ICT-related incident" evidentiary requirements directly.

- **eIDAS 2 (Regulation 2024/1183):** EUDI Wallet Member-State availability target late 2026 (the regulation requires Member States to provide at least one wallet by **end of 2026**). Qualified Trust Service Provider (qTSP) integration for advanced electronic signatures / qualified timestamps is the most relevant integration vector for the v0.5 SCITT emitter — an RFC 3161 TSA from a qTSP gives EU-legal timestamp force.

- **NIS2 Directive (2022/2555):** transposition deadline was 17 October 2024; many Member States have transposed late (Germany, France finalised in 2025; some still pending). EU Commission opened infringement procedures against multiple Member States in late 2024 / early 2025. The November 2025 Digital Omnibus also targets NIS2 amendments (single reporting portal across NIS2 / GDPR / CRA / DORA).

- **GDPR Article 32 (security of processing):** Article 32 obligations bite *concurrently* with AI Act Article 12. Where audit logs contain personal data, Article 5 (data minimisation) and Article 17 (right to erasure) create a hard tension with append-only hash chains — **pg_qsag_audit must store hashes of personal data, not personal data itself**, in the chain, and keep the underlying personal data in a separately-erasable side store keyed by content-hash. This is not new but bears explicit ADR documentation.

 - **Ultralytics YOLOv8 (Dec 2024)**, **Bitwarden CLI 2026.4.0** (compromised npm preinstall hook bootstrapping a Bun runtime + 9.7 MB credential stealer targeting `~/.claude.json` and MCP configs, exfiltrating to a `audit.checkmarx.cx` impersonation domain), **xinference 2.6.x** (two-stage credential stealer on import).
 - **Substrate hardening lessons:**
 (i) **pin all GitHub Actions to commit SHAs, never tags**;
 (ii) require signed commits on all maintainer accounts;
 (iii) use `actions/attest-build-provenance` for every release;
 (iv) reproducibility checks in CI;
 (v) treat any AI-tool config file (`~/.claude.json`, MCP server configs) as a credential surface and avoid storing them in repository checkouts;
 (vi) the `tj-actions` lesson — **a single PAT compromise can rewrite version tags retroactively**, so consumers must pin SHAs and enforce branch protection on tag mutability.

## Per-Topic Confidence and Implementation Implications

### A. SHA3-384 as chain-hash (ADR-0001 §2.3 / §4)
- **Confidence:** HIGH. SHA3-384 is the only NIST-standard hash that retains a 128-bit BHT-quantum collision floor and is in CNSA 2.0's internal-use allow-list. FIPS 202 algorithmically frozen since 2015; the 2025 update is editorial only. Keccak team has reaffirmed no algorithmic changes since round 2.
- **Trajectory 2027–2030:** stable. No NIST signal of SHA-3 deprecation. SP 800-185 streaming revision is forward-compatible.
- **Recommendation:** Lock SHA3-384 for v0.1; design ADR §2.6 to allow algorithm migration via a domain-separation tag inside `dsep_hash` (see §C below).

### B. Dispatcher state-machine routing (ADR-0001 §2.2 / CSWP-39 §3.2.3)
- **Confidence:** HIGH on requirement (CSWP-39 §3.2.3 final, December 2025); MEDIUM on implementation pattern (CSWP-39 is a survey paper, not normative).
- **What CSWP-39 §3.2.3 specifically requires:**
 1. Algorithm negotiation must be integrity-protected (downgrade resistance).
 2. The integrity-protecting algorithm must be at-least-as-strong-as the algorithms it negotiates.
 3. Implementations must provide a way to determine when deployments have shifted from old to new algorithms (auditability of the negotiation outcome).
- **Concrete v0.1 implementation:**
 - Every audit-row insertion logs the negotiated algorithm identifier (`alg_id` column: `'pgcrypto-sha3-384'` | `'plpgsql-fallback-sha3-384'` | future `'pgcrypto-sha3-512'`).
 - The chain-link computation includes `alg_id` in `dsep_hash`: `dsep_hash = SHA3-384(b"q-sag-audit:v1:" || alg_id || canonical_payload)`.
 - The dispatcher logs at session start and at every dispatch decision (not just on first call) — CSWP-39 §3.2.3 explicitly warns against single-point negotiation that is not re-validated.
 - Policy/mechanism separation: which algorithm to use is a GUC (`pg_qsag_audit.preferred_alg`), not a hardcoded constant.

### C. Chain-hash linkage formula `H_n = SHA3-384(H_{n-1} || H_event_n)` where `H_event_n = SHA3-384(canonical_payload || dsep_hash)` (ADR-0001 §2.3)
- **Confidence:** HIGH on the basic linkage shape (this is the standard tamper-evident-log construction post-Crosby-Wallach 2009); MEDIUM on the specific `dsep_hash` semantics.
- **Risk:** if `dsep_hash` is a static constant, a future hash-algorithm migration (SHA3-384 → SHA3-512 or → KMAC256) creates a chain-discontinuity that cannot be cryptographically reasoned about — verifiers will see an "old chain" and a "new chain" that share no structural binding.
- **Recommendation:** make `dsep_hash` a **typed algorithm tag** of the form `SHA3-384(b"q-sag-audit:v1:" || alg_id || canonical_payload)` so a v2 chain can include a `migration anchor row` whose previous-hash field is the v1 `H_n` and whose `dsep_hash` declares the new algorithm. This is structurally identical to RFC 9162's "consistency proof" between two log states. *This change to `dsep_hash` semantics should be made now, in v0.1, because it is non-breaking for the chain and forward-compatible for v0.5+ migrations.*

### D. v0.5 SCITT Signed Statement emitter trajectory (ADR-0001 §2.5 / §2.6)
- **Confidence:** HIGH for the architectural fit, MEDIUM for spec-stability timing.
- **Where SCITT lands by v0.5 release (target: late 2026 / H1 2027):** architecture-22 likely still in IETF Last Call or recently RFC; COSE Receipts (`merkle-tree-proofs-18`) likely RFC; SCRAPI in WGLC. **Low risk of breaking changes** between draft-22 and final RFC based on cadence of -16 to -22.
- **Recommendation for v0.5:** emit COSE Sign1 envelopes per architecture-22; signatures with **ML-DSA-65** (Asqav alignment) for primary, **ES384** (CNSA 2.0 classical-hybrid alignment) as a co-signature. Receipts via the CCF profile (Microsoft CCF reference) or RFC 9162 profile (Sigstore Rekor v2 alignment). Use `did:web` Issuer IDs for the hosted Q-SAG transparency service, X.509 trust-anchor for enterprise customers.

### E. ADR-0001 §2.6 out-of-scope items
The eleven items reserved as out-of-scope-for-v0.1 in ADR-0001 §2.6 (Merkle accumulator, COSE Receipts emitter, SCITT emitter, pgrx-native canonicalisation, EU AI Act export views, CRA connector, ML-DSA / SLH-DSA signing, constant-time hashing, HSM integration, PG <14 support, permissive-mode chain operations) are *consistent* with the research findings. No item flagged for re-prioritisation. The v0.5 SCITT emitter remains the single largest deferred work item; H1 2027 remains a defensible target.

### Contradictions with ADR-0001 — none material; three alignments to incorporate
1. **SHA3-384 chain-hash:** *consistent* with all current research, CNSA 2.0 internal-use allowance, and BHT-quantum analysis. No contradiction.
2. **Dispatcher CSWP-39 §3.2.3 binding:** *consistent* but underspecified — see §B above for the three concrete hardenings.
3. **Hash linkage `dsep_hash` semantics:** *consistent* in shape but should be tightened to a typed algorithm tag — see §C above. This is the single most important pre-v0.1 change.

## Recommendations — Staged, Concrete, Decision-Ready

### Stage 0 — Immediate (May–June 2026, before v0.1 tag)
1. **Tighten `dsep_hash`** to include a typed algorithm tag (`b"q-sag-audit:v1:" || alg_id || canonical_payload`) so the chain is migration-ready. Cost: ~1 day of code + tests. Threshold to revise: any sign of a 2027 NIST hash-deprecation signal (none currently exists; this is purely defensive).

Cost: ~2 days.
3. **Dispatcher logs negotiated `alg_id` into every audit row** (per CSWP-39 §3.2.3). Cost: ~1 day.
4. **Pin every GitHub Action to commit SHA, not tag** (post-tj-actions). Cost: ~1 hour.

## Caveats

1. **Digital Omnibus on AI is unresolved as of the cut-off.** A 2 December 2027 deferral of EU AI Act Article 12 high-risk obligations is *probable but not guaranteed* (third trilogue 13 May 2026, provisional Council agreement 7 May 2026 reported but not formally adopted; original 2 August 2026 deadline still controlling). All compliance language in this synthesis assumes the original deadline. Re-validate after trilogue concludes.

2. **The eleven specific items in ADR-0001 §2.6** are publicly enumerated in the actual ADR file, not inferred. The §E recommendations were cross-checked against the actual ADR-0001-design.md content at commit `e3c91e6`. Any future synthesis update should re-verify against the current ADR text.

3. **Sigstore Rekor v2 PQC support is roadmap-listed but not GA.** The v0.5 SCITT emitter design assumes Rekor v2 supports either ML-DSA or COSE Sign1 with ML-DSA at receipt registration time — this is *probable* by H1 2027 but not certain. Build with an algorithm-abstraction layer (the dispatcher pattern again) so swapping back to ECDSA-P384 at receipt-issue time is not a re-architecture.

4. **CNSA 2.0 SHA3-384 internal-use carve-out** is the basis for retaining SHA3-384 as the chain hash. NSA could narrow this carve-out via a future CSI bulletin; monitor `media.defense.gov` / NSA cybersecurity advisories for CNSA 2.0 FAQ updates. Probability: low through 2028.

5. **pgrx pre-1.0 status** means a pgrx version bump can require non-trivial code changes between minors. Budget for this every ~6 months. A pgrx 1.0.0 release would be a substrate stability event; no public signal as of May 2026 for when it ships.

6. **PGXN v2 RFC-2 / RFC-3 / RFC-5** are pull requests, not ratified specs. Trunk format is *de-facto* via Tembo's 237-extension registry but *de-jure* still pending. The v0.1 ship plan should not block on RFC-5 (release certification with JWS-signed META.json) — that RFC has not been formally filed.

7. **Quantum cryptanalysis advances are an active research area.** The 6-round Keccak attacks of 2022–2025 are not on full 24-round SHA3-384, but they erode safety margin. SHA3-384 remains the post-quantum-conservative choice for the substrate horizon (2027–2030); a 2031+ horizon may want a SHA3-512 or KMAC256 migration anchor — which is exactly why the §C `dsep_hash` typed-tag tightening matters now.

8. **Some industry artefacts cited (Microsoft Agent Governance Toolkit, Asqav SDK)** are recently released (April 2026) and have ~1 month of production track record at the cut-off. Their architectural fit is documented but their resilience to adversarial deployment is not yet demonstrated.

9. **The Madhwal et al. BIOTC 2021 follow-up search was inconclusive.** No subsequent publications by the same authors on PostgreSQL-resident audit chains were found. Absence of evidence is not evidence of absence; a more thorough academic search via DBLP / Google Scholar with a longer time budget would be advisable before claiming "no follow-up exists" in any external publication.

10. **All regulatory citations are good-faith summaries of the legal text and contemporaneous secondary sources.** They are not legal advice. Substrate compliance posture should be confirmed by counsel admitted in each relevant jurisdiction (EU, UK, US federal, US state, AU, JP, KR, SG) before any compliance claim is made in marketing or customer-facing documentation.
